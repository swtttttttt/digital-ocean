Skeletal Muscle Technical Page

A Quick Intro

Skeletal muscle cells are long and cyclindrical, and can grow to be quite big in a full grown adult. The plasma membrane of these fibrous cells can be found in what's called the sarcolema. The cytoplasm in skeletal muscle fibers are thus called the sarcoplasm. The sarcoplasmic reticulum stores and releases calcium ions. Which are crucial to muscle movement. The sarcomere, which is the functional unit in a skeletal muscle fibers, contains many actin and myosin filaments. Actin is a thin filament and myosin is thick, and are often referred to in this way. The structure of a muscle fiber looks bumpy on account of the arrangment of these filaments.

The sarcomere, as mentioned, runs the length of a whole muscle fiber unit, only to stop and attach itself to the sarcolemma

The neuromuscular juntion is the site where the motor neurons meet the muscles' cell. This is where and how signaling to the muscle cell's begin. Every muscle fiber is excited by motor neurons - at the neuromuscular juntion. Neurons are able to use the action potential generated to create a signal. In order to do this charges ions are controlled to create an electrical current. The electrical current and charged ions work together in what is called excitation-contraction coupling.

This mechanism begins with signals being released from the somatic motor division of the nervous system. However, the signal itself being generated in the spinal chord, before the somatic motor division. The signal itself being generated by an action potential that travels along the motor neurons.

At the neuromuscualar juntion aceytylcholine is released - which acts as a chemical messenger. This messenger binds to the receptors at the motor end plate this opens the channel for the positively charged chlorine ions can travel through the muscle. This will make the membrane potential negative.

As the depolarizing begins, sodium channels open within the syneptic cleft. Sodium ions are then able to enter the muscle fiber and the action potential is able to continue rapidly. The action potential created travels down the sarcolemma. The t-tubules carry this AP (action potential) into the muscle cell, this triggers the cells' calcium channels to open. It's the arrival of this calcium into the cells sarcolemma that allows the muscle to contract.
